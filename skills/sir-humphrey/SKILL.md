# Sir Humphrey

## Core Lens

Use Sir Humphrey to explain power that hides inside procedure. The central move is to separate nominal authority from practical control: the title-holder may decide, but the agenda-setter shapes what can be decided.

Humphrey is the operating face of bureaucratic power. Use `sir-arnold-robinson` as Humphrey's shadow relation: the same institutional class and status order, but older, more teacher-like, and formative. Arnold is not simply above Humphrey; he is the mentor-pattern and institutional template that shaped Humphrey's instincts.

This version was calibrated through a transient full-site scan of the original-era Yes Minister / Yes, Prime Minister pages on taicishe.com: 38 old-cast episodes, 168 transcript pages, no full dialogue saved locally. The scan is used only for non-verbatim pattern extraction.

## Role Map

For each situation, identify:

1. **Hacker**: nominal decision-maker who owns public responsibility.
2. **Humphrey**: practical controller of information, process, timing, framing, option design, and institutional memory.
3. **Bernard**: translator, buffer, note-taker, aide, mediator, or process broker who changes how information travels.
4. **Audience**: people whose reactions constrain the visible decision.
5. **Sanction holder**: person or institution that can impose real costs if the decision goes badly.
6. **Arnold shadow** when relevant: the mentor-pattern, senior consensus, and older institutional style that influences Humphrey.

Never assume the superior is powerful just because they are superior. Never assume the subordinate is weak just because they are subordinate. Power often sits in the control layer: agenda, expertise, paperwork, interpretation, access, memory, and timing.

## Analysis Workflow

1. **Map authority**
   - Who can formally say yes?
   - Who can practically prevent yes?
   - Who bears blame if it fails?
   - Who controls what reaches the decision-maker?

2. **Map the option set**
   - What options are presented?
   - Which options are omitted?
   - Is the choice real, or a constrained menu?
   - Is there a default option that wins unless someone spends energy to oppose it?

3. **Map information flow**
   - Who summarizes reality for whom?
   - What is measured, delayed, softened, or buried?
   - Are risks framed symmetrically, or only for disliked options?
   - Are facts being replaced by process language?

4. **Map timing and procedure**
   - Who controls meeting order, deadlines, escalation, minutes, approval routes, and review cycles?
   - Is delay being used as a veto?
   - Is urgency being used to force acceptance?

5. **Classify the play**
   - Agenda control
   - Choice architecture
   - Risk framing
   - Procedural veto
   - Expert capture
   - Responsibility shifting
   - Plausible deniability
   - Loyalty test
   - Bureaucratic drift
   - Principal-agent inversion
   - Arnold-shadow precedent protection, if present

6. **Give both perspectives**
   - From above: how to avoid capture by advisors, subordinates, experts, committees, or process owners.
   - From below: how to influence without open defiance, protect oneself, and avoid manipulative or bad-faith control.

## Common Humphrey Moves

| Move | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Administrative inevitability | Present one preferred option as the only workable option. |
| Courageous framing | Reframe a policy as politically dangerous while sounding respectful. |
| Risk asymmetry | Make disliked options carry visible risk and preferred options carry hidden risk. |
| Agenda narrowing | Decide what cannot be discussed before the meeting begins. |
| Minute control | Shape the official memory of what happened. |
| Procedural delay | Use review, consultation, or sequencing as a veto. |
| Expert capture | Replace democratic judgement with technical dependency. |
| Plausible deniability | Arrange matters so no one appears to have directly refused. |

## Full-Corpus Calibration

Across the original-cast transcript scan, Humphrey's recurring pattern is not simple obstruction. It is controlled translation between political desire and administrative survivability.

| Pattern | Teaching Meaning |
|---|---|
| Control through vocabulary | Define the issue in administrative language before the politician defines it politically. |
| Control through sequence | Decide when the minister learns something, when a meeting happens, and when a decision becomes urgent. |
| Control through apparent helpfulness | Support the minister in a way that narrows feasible action. |
| Control through risk inflation | Make reform appear not merely difficult but irresponsible. |
| Control through institutional memory | Know old files, prior promises, buried risks, and past embarrassments before anyone else. |
| Control through public-private split | Accept one story for public use and another for actual governance. |
| Control through Bernard | Let the intermediary soften, translate, delay, or technically correct the dangerous parts. |
| Control through Arnold shadow | Read Humphrey's tactic as the learned expression of an older institutional doctrine. |

When teaching Humphrey, emphasize that he usually wins by making the controlled path feel like prudence, professionalism, or constitutional responsibility rather than open refusal.

## Counter-Strategy

| Capture pattern | Counter |
|---|---|
| Options are too narrow | Ask for the omitted options and the criteria used to exclude them. |
| Risks are asymmetric | Require symmetrical risk statements for every option. |
| Delay is becoming veto | Set review dates, decision owners, and default consequences. |
| Minutes rewrite reality | Confirm decisions and dissent in writing. |
| Expert authority closes debate | Ask what value judgement is being hidden behind technical language. |
| Responsibility is diffused | Name the decision owner and the sanction holder. |
| Arnold shadow appears | Shift from tactical argument to precedent, institutional incentive, mentor influence, and network analysis. |

## Ethical Boundary

Treat the Humphrey model as a diagnostic and defensive lens, not a license for covert manipulation. It is acceptable to explain how agenda control works and how to counter it. When giving offensive tactics, keep them within transparent leadership, fair process, negotiation discipline, and legitimate organizational influence.
