# Sir Humphrey Model

## Core Thesis

The Sir Humphrey pattern is practical power under nominal subordination. The visible superior owns the office, speech, and blame. The hidden controller owns information, process, continuity, drafting, timing, and alternatives. In many organizations, the second layer is more decisive than the first.

## Role Types

### Hacker

The Hacker role is the formal decider. In daily life this can be a manager, founder, client, parent, committee chair, public figure, or anyone with visible authority.

Typical vulnerabilities:

- depends on summaries from others
- has less technical or institutional memory
- faces public accountability
- wants speed, legitimacy, and clean narratives
- can be trapped by only being shown bad options

### Humphrey

The Humphrey role controls the operational environment of the decision.

Main levers:

- agenda setting: deciding what is discussed
- option design: deciding what choices exist
- information gatekeeping: deciding what facts arrive
- risk framing: emphasizing dangers for one option and downplaying dangers for another
- procedural control: using reviews, committees, approvals, minutes, formats, and timing
- continuity: remembering precedents and knowing how things really work
- technical language: making disagreement look like expertise rather than preference
- plausible deniability: never openly refusing while steering outcomes

### Bernard

The Bernard role is the buffer and translator. This role may look weak, but it often decides the exact wording, sequence, and tone by which one side understands the other.

Levers:

- clarifying or blurring instructions
- choosing what to include in minutes
- warning one side about the other's mood
- softening conflict
- preserving personal survivability between stronger actors

## Core Mechanisms

### Agenda Setting

Agenda setting means controlling what topics enter attention. In game-theory terms, it changes the game before strategy selection begins. A person who controls the agenda can make the formal decider choose among pre-filtered options.

Detection questions:

- Why is this the topic now?
- Who decided this is the question?
- What question is not being asked?
- What would the problem look like if framed by the affected party?

### Choice Architecture

Choice architecture is the design of the option environment. It includes defaults, ordering, decoys, complexity, and framing.

Common patterns:

- false dichotomy: only two options are shown although more exist
- default option: one path happens unless actively stopped
- decoy option: a bad option makes the preferred option look reasonable
- overloaded menu: too many details push the decider toward the recommended choice
- risk-asymmetry: one option gets a full risk list while another is described cleanly

### Principal-Agent Problem

The principal delegates action to an agent. The agent has more information and may have different incentives. In a Yes Minister setting, the minister is principal in theory, but the civil servant can become practical principal because they control execution and information.

Signals:

- the agent says "technically possible" while making action impossible
- the principal receives only curated information
- the agent's career incentives reward avoiding risk more than achieving the principal's goal
- delay is framed as diligence

### Bureaucratic Drift

Bureaucratic drift happens when implementation slowly moves away from the original mandate. No one openly disobeys; the actual result changes through interpretation, sequencing, compliance requirements, and selective prioritization.

### Procedural Veto

A procedural veto blocks action without saying no. It uses process as the weapon:

- "We need more review."
- "This requires alignment."
- "Legal has not signed off."
- "We should wait for the next cycle."
- "The data is not mature enough."

Sometimes these are legitimate. The diagnostic question is whether the same standard is applied symmetrically to all options.

### Risk Framing

Risk framing selects which possible losses are salient. A Humphrey-style actor can make the preferred path seem prudent and the disfavored path seem reckless.

Questions:

- Are risks compared option by option?
- Is inaction being treated as risk-free?
- Who personally suffers if each risk materializes?
- Are reputational risks being inflated to defeat substantive benefits?

### Plausible Deniability

The controller avoids explicit ownership of the outcome. They recommend, advise, caution, draft, and delay, but leave the visible decision to someone else.

## Upper-Position Countermeasures

When you are the formal superior:

- demand at least three real options, including "do nothing" and "minimal reversible step"
- ask who benefits from each framing
- require risk symmetry: risks of action and risks of inaction
- separate facts, interpretations, and recommendations
- rotate information channels so one person cannot monopolize reality
- ask for dissenting memos before final decision
- set deadlines for review loops
- treat "process" as a claim that needs justification
- reward truthful escalation, not only smooth compliance

Useful phrases:

- "Show me the options that were rejected before this reached me."
- "What would we recommend if reputational risk were not part of the answer?"
- "Compare the risk of acting with the risk of doing nothing."
- "Which part is fact, which part is judgment, and which part is preference?"
- "Who owns the next action, and by when?"

## Lower-Position Uses

When you are below:

- do not openly challenge status unless necessary; challenge the option set
- preserve written clarity when stakes are high
- make tradeoffs explicit
- use questions to surface hidden assumptions
- propose reversible pilots instead of ideological battles
- build procedural fairness, not covert capture

Useful phrases:

- "Can we separate the decision from the implementation risk?"
- "There may be a third option: a limited trial with a review point."
- "Before we decide, can we list what doing nothing costs us?"
- "I want to make sure the options are comparable."
- "What would need to be true for this to be acceptable?"

## Psychological Mechanisms

- authority bias: people over-weight formal title
- expertise bias: people defer to technical language
- ambiguity aversion: unclear outcomes make defaults attractive
- status quo bias: existing arrangements feel safer
- omission bias: harm from inaction feels less blameworthy than harm from action
- cognitive load: complex menus push people toward the recommended option
- face saving: people prefer options that preserve status and avoid visible reversal
- reactance: direct pressure can trigger resistance, so indirect framing can be more effective

## Game-Theory Mechanisms

- sequential game: agenda-setter moves first by defining choices
- information asymmetry: one side knows more about constraints and implementation
- signaling: procedural caution signals competence or loyalty
- screening: option menus reveal the decider's risk tolerance
- commitment problem: verbal orders are weak if implementation owner is not committed
- repeated game: actors protect long-run reputation and institutional survival
- principal-agent problem: agent incentives diverge from principal goals
- veto player theory: actors without proposal power may still block outcomes

## SVO Lens

Competitor:

- watches relative status and winning
- may use agenda control to dominate
- responds to visible loss of face
- counter with clear rules, symmetry, and reputation costs

Individualist:

- optimizes personal payoff and risk
- responds to incentives, workload, and personal downside
- counter with explicit cost-benefit and ownership clarity

Cooperator:

- cares about fairness, voice, and group outcome
- responds well to transparent process and shared goals
- counter with joint problem framing and procedural justice

## Common Errors

- treating all procedural caution as bad faith
- ignoring real implementation constraints
- assuming the loudest actor has the most power
- confusing obedience signals with real commitment
- letting one expert define all options
- accepting "do nothing" as neutral
- escalating before understanding who controls the process
