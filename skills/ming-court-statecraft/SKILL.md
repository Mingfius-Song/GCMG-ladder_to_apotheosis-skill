# Ming Court Statecraft

## Core Rule

Use this skill only as an enhancement package for `humphrey-social-strategy` and `sir-humphrey`. It must not replace the Sir Humphrey axis.

Sir Humphrey explains present-tense bureaucratic control: agenda, options, procedure, information, timing, minutes, risk framing, and plausible deniability. Ming court statecraft adds an eastern imperial-bureaucratic layer: sovereign authority, grand-secretariat mediation, inner-court access, censorial morality, fiscal constraint, ritual legitimacy, factional pressure, and reform aftershocks.

Do not copy, quote, summarize chapter-by-chapter, or reconstruct copyrighted modern books. Use non-verbatim structural distillation. Treat modern works such as *Ming Dynasty popular histories* and *1587, A Year of No Significance / Wanli Shiwunian* as thematic signals, not as text sources to reproduce.

## Trigger Patterns

Use this lens when the user asks about:

- A nominally supreme actor who is constrained by files, rituals, ministers, advisors, factions, public morality, or inherited institutions.
- Strong agents who serve the ruler while accumulating independent power.
- Reformers who improve the system but are punished after losing their patron or mandate.
- Moral language used as a weapon: "principle", "ancestral rule", "public righteousness", "loyalty", "orthodoxy", "clean hands".
- Inner-court vs outer-court dynamics: access to the ruler, private channels, secretaries, eunuchs, assistants, gatekeepers, family members, trusted aides.
- Censorial or public-opinion attacks that turn technical decisions into legitimacy trials.
- Fiscal bottlenecks behind grand political language.
- Factional conflict where every practical proposal becomes a test of loyalty.
- Ming figures or archetypes: Wanli, Zhang Juzheng, Shen Shixing, Hai Rui, Qi Jiguang, Li Zhi, Yan Song, Xu Jie, Gao Gong, Feng Bao, Wei Zhongxian, Donglin, Chongzhen.

Do not use it merely because the situation is "Chinese" or "ancient". Use it when the structure matches.

## Core Analysis Map

For each relevant case, add only the necessary Ming-statecraft categories:

1. **Throne / nominal sovereignty** - who seems to hold final authority?
2. **Grand-secretariat layer** - who converts messy reality into options the ruler can approve?
3. **Inner-court access** - who controls proximity, mood, timing, private channels, and last-mile interpretation?
4. **Outer-court legitimacy** - which formal offices, censors, experts, or moral communities can challenge the action?
5. **Fiscal and logistical constraint** - what money, grain, staffing, military, or execution constraint makes the grand idea fragile?
6. **Moralized frame** - what language turns a tradeoff into righteousness, betrayal, loyalty, or orthodoxy?
7. **Reform aftershock** - who benefits from reversal after the patron, crisis, or mandate disappears?
8. **Historical rhyme** - which Ming archetype clarifies the mechanism without forcing a one-to-one analogy?

## Common Archetypes

| Archetype | Mechanism | Modern analogue |
|---|---|---|
| Wanli pattern | Nominal center resists a system it cannot openly defeat, then uses withdrawal or inaction. | Leader with formal authority but low trust in the bureaucracy. |
| Zhang Juzheng pattern | Strong agent centralizes execution under a young or dependent sovereign, reforms effectively, then becomes vulnerable after patronage ends. | Powerful COO/chief of staff/minister whose success creates future retaliation. |
| Shen Shixing pattern | Moderate mediator tries to keep peace among incompatible pressures. | Coordinator who preserves continuity but cannot solve structural contradiction. |
| Hai Rui pattern | Moral capital is extremely high, but operational adaptability is low. | Clean critic whose standards expose rot but do not build a coalition to govern. |
| Qi Jiguang pattern | Professional competence depends on political protection, funding, and institutional tolerance. | Expert operator whose real capability is constrained by budget and politics. |
| Yan Song / Xu Jie pattern | Long patience, factional timing, and legitimacy reversal decide court survival. | Insider rivalry where removal depends on timing and moral framing. |
| Wei Zhongxian pattern | Proximity to the sovereign and control of access become a parallel authority system. | Gatekeeper captures the principal through scheduling, information, and fear. |
| Chongzhen pattern | High suspicion and urgent correction destroy trust, continuity, and execution capacity. | Leader overcorrects by rotating agents too fast and punishing the only people who can act. |
